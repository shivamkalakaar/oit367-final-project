## Tail-Risk Behavior

A central question for Ethisphere's value proposition is whether honorees provide downside protection when it matters most — during the worst market months. We define "tail months" as those in the bottom 5th percentile of benchmark (SGMACUT Index) monthly returns and ask three questions: (1) how much less do honorees fall on average, (2) how often does the typical honoree outperform the benchmark, and (3) what share of individual honorees beat the benchmark in those months?

**Method.** We compute monthly returns for all 113 honoree stocks from daily Capital IQ price data and for the SGMACUT total return benchmark from January 2021 through December 2025 (59 months). Applying a 5th-percentile threshold identifies the three worst benchmark months: April 2022 (−7.93%), June 2022 (−8.56%), and September 2022 (−9.62%) — all occurring during the 2022 rate-hiking selloff. For each tail month, we calculate the excess return of every honoree relative to the benchmark. We then construct 95% bootstrap confidence intervals by resampling the set of honorees with replacement across 10,000 iterations, computing the average excess return, hit rate, and breadth for each bootstrap sample.

**Results.** Honorees outperformed the benchmark in all three tail months. The average excess return was +1.39 percentage points, with a 95% CI of [+0.50, +2.28] pp — entirely above zero. The hit rate (share of honoree-month observations beating the benchmark) was 59.3% [53.1%, 65.2%], and breadth (average share of honorees outperforming in a given tail month) was also 59.3% [53.1%, 65.2%]. At the individual month level, April 2022 showed the strongest effect (+2.50 pp excess, 62.8% of honorees outperforming), while June 2022 was the weakest (+0.18 pp, 53.1%).

As a robustness check, we repeat the analysis at the 10th percentile (6 tail months). The effect persists but attenuates: average excess return falls to +0.82 pp [+0.22, +1.42], hit rate to 55.6% [51.2%, 60.0%], and breadth to 55.6% [51.2%, 60.0%]. The lower CI bounds remain above zero for excess return and above 50% for hit rate, confirming the finding is not an artifact of the 5% threshold.

**Limitations.** Our sample contains only 59 months and 3 tail events at the 5% level, all concentrated in 2022. While bootstrap CIs account for cross-sectional uncertainty across honorees, they do not address the small number of tail periods. We cannot rule out that the 2022 selloff — driven primarily by interest rate sensitivity — favored the sector composition of honorees rather than their ethical profile per se. Future work could decompose the tail-risk effect into sector-level contributions using the firmographic data.
